Drevenmüir Rvun

Drevenmüir Rvun, or simply Drevenmüir, is the language spoken by the people living on the Drevenmüir Penninsula. It is split into 3 dialects: Standard Drevenmüir, Southern Drevenmüir, and Low Drevenmüir. In addition, many important texts are still written in the literary dialect of Old Drevenmüir.

Demographic and ethnographic information

Overview

The Drevenmüir dialects of Standard and Southern are spoken by the Dwarves living in Drevenmüir. Standard Drevenmüir is predominantly spoken by the Dwarves living in and near the Hrkerghrgän. Conversely, Southern Drevenmüir is spoken by the Dwarves living on the Asa Füle. The other dialect, Low Drevenmüir, otherwise known as "Naashi Drawammwii" by it's speakers is much more divergent from the Standard dialect than the Southern Dialect. This language is mainly spoken by the goblins that have immigrated to Drevenmüir from the Gnarsh. Because of it's divergence and social status, many speakers of this dialect also speak the Standard dialect.

Phrases to know for travellers!

With just these simple phrases, you should have a much easier time navigating around the continent.

  1. Gnacht elen - Hello
  2. De zai ölen - Goodbye
  3. Ze älojje elen - I'm sorry
  4. Schige dwä - Thank you
  5. Zer rvojjej - You're welcome
  6. Rvöuv pföu zacge dwä? - How much does that cost?
  7. Ze rvöuv zalscheñ dwä? - May I have that?
  8. LOCATION pföus ere? - Where is LOCATION?

History

Old Drevenmüir was first spoken around year 3,500. Uhrkik was spoken around year 1000. Proto-Anebasi was spoken around year 0. The 3 Drevenmüir dialects started diverging around 500 years ago, with the immigration of the Gnoblins to the Drevenmüir Penninsula.

Morphological typology

Drevenmüir is prodominantly an isolating language. However, the language features inflection and derivation. All nouns inflect for case and their class. All verbs also inflect for person, tense, and number. Derivational operations are also very common.

The language is also predominantly head-initial.

Noun and noun-phrase operations

Compounding

When nouns are compounded together, the class prefix of the noun placed first is removed and the class prefix of the second noun is placed in front. Similarly, when the compound is of a verb and a noun of an adjective and a noun, the noun's class prefix is placed in front.

flükshem (coal) + ezchk (bird) = ezhschenk (raven)
schal (white) + ezchk (bird) = ezhschalk (swan)

Older compunds may not follow this pattern, but this is the general rule for forming new compounds.

Compounding is relitvely common in Drevenmüir, with many words being made up of two or more roots. However, many words are also derived by just changing the class prefix, and this is arguablely more common.

Nominalization and denominalization

Nominalization is a very common process in Drevenmüir. All adjectives and verbs can be turned into nouns. This process is used when the speaker refers to something by it's descriptors. This process is extremely productive. When a noun is produced in this fasion, stress is never put on the class prefix.

kal (black) + IN = rukál (black thing)
kal (black) + WL = ezchkál (black fish/bird)
pfarden + AN = lspfarden (person who fights)

Denominalization is not very common in Drevenmüir, but it does happen in a few words. When a word is denominalized, the prefix is removed. For example, compare älpfarden (war) wtih pfarden "to fight". However, most of the time nouns are used as verbs or adjectives their prefix stays. The demoninalization process is not very productive in modern Drevenmüir.

Number

Number is marked by modifying the class prefix. Drevenmüir distinguishes between singular and plural when the noun is definite. However, this distinction is only for a handful of noun classes- DA, DW, EX, WL, AN, SL, UN, SP, MA, KA. In these cases, marking number is obligitory. Otherwise, nouns are not marked for number. If plurality needs to be specified when it cannot be indicated by the class prefix, a number word is used.

Case

Drevenmüir features 6 cases: nominative, accusative, dative, genitive, instrumental, and locative.

The nominative case marks the subject of a sentence. This case takes no marking.

The accusative case marks the subject of a sentence. This case usually takes no marking, but if the final consonant is an alveolar stop it becomes a palatal stop, and if the final consonant is a vowel -j is appendend. In addition, if the word ends with /ə/, the final vowel would become /ej/.

The dative case marks most indirect objects. This is marked by the suffix -eñ, or just -ñ after vowels.

The genitive case marks nouns that posess other nouns. In addition, this is used with some prepositions. This is marked by the suffix -er, or just -r after vowels.

The instrumental case marks nouns the action is completed with and time words. This is marked by the suffix -ch.

The locative case marks places the subject is at. This is marked by the suffix -s, or -sch after palatal consonants.

Cgorch ze schlükpasen älkermigher gnemüicheñ schries beretalen.
night-INS 1asg-NOM DEF.letter-ACC DEF.enemy-GEN DEF.spy-DAT DEF.city-LOC give-1asg.PST
At night, I gave the enemy's spy the letter in the city.

Posession

Posession is shown by placing the posessor in the genitive case. There is no agreement that takes place. In addition, there are no differences between how different types of posession are expressed. The posessor is always placed directly before the noun it posesses.

Articles, determiners, and demonstratives

Drevenmüir has no articles, but noun class prefixes have a similar function. These indicate definiteness and number based on their declension.

In addition, there is also one demonstrative. There is no distinction between degrees of distance. However, class is distingished. The demonstrative öuv must always take the prefix of the class of the object being referred to. The demonstrative is also always in the definite form, except for class TI. Number is also distingished if the class of the object being referred to distinguishes number.

One special case to note is class TI nouns. These nouns cannot take the expected demonstrative. Instead, this class uses the demonstrative jontach.

Ñöunöuv züper isch!
KA-DEM delicious COP.3asg
This/that (food) is delicious!

If the class of the object being referred to is unknown, speakers will commonly use class IN, or any other that seems to fit.

Unlike English, demonstratives are not usually used directly before another noun (ex. "this table"). In that case, just the demonstrative would be used, or just noun in definite form.

Diminuation and augmentation

Diminuation is shown by placing nouns into class SL. Agumentation is shown by placing nouns in Class ES or Class EX.

Ghrgän "Mountain" + Class SL = Chengän "Hill"
Ghrgän "Mountain" + Class ES = Ñegngän "Large mountain"
Ghrgän "Mountain" + Class EX = Drgän "Exalted mountain"

Placing a noun in class SL can also show endearment towards it. Placing a noun into ES, and especially EX, indicates respect for the object.

If there's a situation where placing the noun into one of these classes would create ambiguity, the class prefix can be added infront of the other class prefix. The second class prefix will always be in the indefinite form. This strategy can also be used if it's wanted to diminish a noun in class SL or augment a noun in class ES/EX.

Ñöunmíl "The flower" + Class SL = Schenmíl "The little flower"
*Rvemíl "The wilted flower" + Class SL = Schenmíl "The little flower"
Rvemíl "The wilted flower" + Class SL = Schenrumíl "The little wilted flower"

Pragmatically marked structures

Non-declarative speech acts

The particle che marks a sentence is an interrogative question. In addition, a rising intonation pattern in used.

De Drevenmüir Rvuñ rwunch che?
2asg.NOM Drevenmüir DEF.Language.ACC speak-2asg.PRS QP
Do you speak Drevenmüirish?

For affirmative questions, the negation particle kal is added to the end of the sentence and a rising intonation is used.

De Drevenmüir Rvuñ rwunch kal?
2asg.NOM Drevenmüir DEF.Language.ACC speak-2asg.PRS NEG
You speak Drevenmüirish, don't you?

The particle dwä is mostly used to make a sentence more polite, similar to "please" in English. Unlike English, this particle can be used at the end of most sentences. If the verb of the clause is in the future tense, the meaning changes. In this case, the particle would be used to request action. A rising intonation pattern is only used when this particle is used in the second use.

Ze rvöuv zalscheñ dwä?
1asg.NOM IN-one.ACC have-1asg.FUT PROP
May I have that?

When question words are used, they are placed in the position of the object of the sentence. A list of question words can be found below.

Lspöu - who
Pföu - what
Jonpöu - when
Pföus - where
Pföural - why
Pföuwaj - how

Rvöuv pföu zacge che?
IN-one.NOM what cost-3isg.PRS QP
What does that cost?
